Buying Guide

Phone Buyer's Guide

From budget workhorses to flagship cameras — find the right phone for your needs and budget.

Counterfeit Phones Are Rampant: Fake iPhones and Samsung Galaxies are common on third-party marketplaces. Signs: the OS feels different (custom Android skin on a fake iPhone), cameras are misaligned, screen resolution is lower than advertised. Always activate and verify IMEI through the manufacturer before paying.

Budget Tier — $150 to $300

Best for students, seniors, or anyone who needs a capable phone without breaking the bank. These phones trade camera quality and premium materials for solid performance and long battery life.

  • Google Pixel 7a / 8a — Best camera in the budget tier. Clean Android, guaranteed updates. Refurbished units hit $250.
  • Samsung Galaxy A35 / A55 — Solid all-rounders with good displays and 4+ years of software support.
  • Motorola Moto G Stylus / G Power — Headphone jack, SD card slot, enormous batteries. Perfect media consumption devices.
  • Nothing Phone (2a) — Unique design, near-stock Android, surprisingly good performance for $300.

Mid-Range — $400 to $700

The sweet spot for most people. You get 90% of flagship features — good cameras, 120 Hz OLED displays, water resistance, and wireless charging — at half the price.

Flagship — $800+

Best cameras, brightest screens, fastest processors, and maximum build quality. These phones last 4–6 years with proper care. If you keep a phone for 5+ years, the higher upfront cost is justified.

  • iPhone 16 Pro / Pro Max — Best video recording on any phone. A18 Pro chip, 5x optical zoom, titanium frame. $999+.
  • Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra — S Pen, best Android display, 200 MP camera with exceptional zoom. $1,299+.
  • Google Pixel 9 Pro — Best computational photography, 7 years of OS updates, excellent AI tools. $999+.
  • OnePlus 13 — Snapdragon 8 Gen 4, 120W charging, IP68. The value flagship at $899.
Trade-In Tips: Samsung and Google offer the best trade-in values (often $400–600 for a 2-year-old flagship). iPhones hold resale value best — a 3-year-old iPhone still fetches $300–500 on Swappa. Always check carrier trade-in deals around release season (Sept–Oct for iPhone, Jan–Feb for Galaxy).

Carrier Compatibility

Not all phones work on all carriers. Check compatibility before buying, especially if importing. The US market has three major network technologies:

Avoiding Counterfeit Phones

  • Check the IMEI. Use the manufacturer's warranty checker (Samsung, Apple, Google) to verify the IMEI is legitimate and not blacklisted.
  • Inspect the display. Counterfeit OLEDs have poor viewing angles, uneven brightness, and thick bezels.
  • Test the cameras. Fake phones often use a single sensor behind multiple lens cutouts. Cover each lens — if a "lens" is decorative, it is fake.
  • Verify the OS. On Android, go to Settings > About Phone and check the model number against the manufacturer's official list.
  • Buy unlocked. Factory-unlocked phones directly from Apple, Google, or Samsung are guaranteed genuine and carrier-compatible.

eSIM, Physical SIM & 5G Bands

Most 2026 flagships are eSIM-only in the US (iPhone 16, Pixel 9 series). If you travel internationally, a phone with a physical SIM slot is still more convenient for using local prepaid SIMs. Check 5G band support: US carriers use n5, n41, n71, n77, n260. A phone sold in Asia may miss mmWave (n260/n261), which means slower 5G on Verizon. GSMArena publishes accurate band specs for every model.

Refurbished & Open-Box: Manufacturer-refurbished phones (Apple Certified Refurbished, Samsung Certified Re-Newed) are identical to new units with full warranty, often 15–20% cheaper. Open-box units from Best Buy are also reliable. Avoid "seller refurbished" on Amazon — quality is inconsistent.
