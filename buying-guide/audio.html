Audio Buyer's Guide

IEMs, headphones, speakers, DACs, and amps — navigate the world of personal audio with confidence.

Counterfeit Audio Gear: Fake AirPods, Beats, and IEMs (especially popular Chi-Fi brands like Moondrop, 7Hz, Truthear) are everywhere on Amazon and AliExpress. Signs: mismatched serial numbers, poor print quality on packaging, thinner cables, and notably worse sound quality. Always buy directly from the manufacturer's official store or authorized distributors like Linsoul, ShenzhenAudio, or headphones.com.

IEMs (In-Ear Monitors) — $20 to $500+

IEMs offer the best price-to-performance ratio in personal audio. A $50 IEM today matches the sound quality of $200 headphones from a decade ago. Great for commuting, gaming (excellent imaging), and critical listening.

Headphones — Open vs Closed Back

Open-back headphones have a wider soundstage and more natural timbre but leak sound both ways — not suitable for noisy environments or shared spaces. Closed-back headphones isolate you from ambient noise and prevent sound leakage but often have a smaller soundstage and can sound "trapped."

  • Open-Back — Sennheiser HD 560S ($150) — Best entry into open-back. Neutral, detailed, comfortable. Excellent for mixing.
  • Open-Back — Hifiman Sundara ($250) — Planar magnetic, exceptional clarity and bass extension. Great for critical listening.
  • Open-Back — Beyerdynamic DT 900 Pro X ($250) — Slightly more treble-forward. Good for analytical listening and gaming.
  • Closed-Back — Audio-Technica ATH-M50x ($150) — Industry standard for monitoring. V-shaped sound, foldable. Good for portable use.
  • Closed-Back — Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro ($160) — Excellent isolation, great for tracking and noisy environments.
  • Closed-Back — Fiio FT1 ($150) — Newer closed-back with excellent bass and detail. Good value.

Speakers — $100 to $1,500+

Nearfield monitors for desktop use, bookshelf speakers for small rooms, and floor-standing towers for dedicated listening spaces. Active speakers (built-in amp) are simpler; passive speakers require a separate amplifier.

  • Edifier R1280DBs ($120) — Best budget active speakers. Bluetooth, optical, sub out. Good for desktop use.
  • Vanatoo Transparent One Encore ($500) — Excellent active nearfield monitors with DSP tuning. Great for mixing and casual listening.
  • Kali Audio LP-6 V2 ($300/pair) — Accurate studio monitors. Neutral response, good bass extension. Best for production.
  • Klipsch RP-600M II + Aiyima A07 amp ($500–600) — Excellent passive bookshelf setup. Lively, engaging sound.
  • JBL 305P MkII + LSR310S sub ($400–550) — Full-range studio monitoring. The industry standard for budget production.

DACs & Amps — When Do You Need One?

Modern laptops and phones have decent DACs, but external DACs/amps become necessary when: (1) you hear hiss or static from your motherboard audio, (2) your headphones are hard to drive (high impedance — over 100 ohms), or (3) you want to use balanced cables for lower noise floor.

Impedance & Sensitivity: Low-impedance headphones (16–32Ω) are easy to drive from phones/laptops. High-impedance headphones (120–600Ω) need dedicated amps. Sensitivity (dB/mW) matters too — higher sensitivity = louder at the same power. Rule of thumb: over 100Ω or under 98 dB sensitivity = get an amp.

Wired vs Wireless

Wired — no latency, no batteries, unlimited audio quality. Best for gaming, production, and critical listening. Wireless — convenience of no cables. LDAC (Sony) and aptX Lossless (Qualcomm) can approach wired quality under ideal conditions. For general use, a $30–50 TWS earbud (Moondrop Golden Ages, Galaxy Buds FE) is good enough. For gaming or music production, always go wired.
