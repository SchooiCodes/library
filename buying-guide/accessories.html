Accessories Buyer's Guide

Keyboards, mice, webcams, microphones, docks, and cables — the gear that completes your setup.

Counterfeit & Substandard Accessories: Fake mechanical keyboards with non-functional switches, counterfeit Logitech mice, and dangerous USB-C cables that lack proper e-markers are widespread. USB cables that claim "240W" charging without proper certification can destroy your devices. Only buy from the manufacturer or authorized distributors.

Keyboards — Mechanical Tiers

Mechanical keyboards fall into three broad tiers. Pre-built boards are fine for most users, but the custom mechanical keyboard hobby has dramatically improved the quality of budget options in recent years.

Mice — $15 to $150

The right mouse depends on grip style (palm, claw, fingertip) and hand size. Gaming mice emphasize low weight (<60 g for competitive FPS), high polling rates (1000–8000 Hz), and optical switches. Office mice prioritize battery life and ergonomics.

  • Logitech G305 Lightspeed ($35–50) — Wireless, 12,000 DPI, 250 h battery. Best budget wireless gaming mouse.
  • Razer DeathAdder V3 ($60–80) — 59 g, ergonomic, Focus Pro 30K sensor. Great for palm grip.
  • Logitech G Pro X Superlight 2 ($120–140) — 60 g, Hero 2 sensor, 2000 Hz polling. The competitive FPS standard.
  • Razer Viper Mini Signature Edition ($280) — 49 g, magnesium alloy shell, 8000 Hz polling. Extreme lightweight.
  • Logitech MX Master 3S ($80–100) — 8,000 DPI, silent clicks, horizontal scroll. Best productivity mouse.
  • Keychron M3 / M4 ($40–60) — Lightweight, good sensors, USB-C. Solid budget option.

Microphones — $20 to $300

  • Samson Go Mic ($35) — Clip-on portable USB mic. Good for calls and portable recording.
  • Blue Yeti / Yeti X ($80–130) — USB condenser, multiple polar patterns. Good for podcasting and single-person use. Picks up background noise.
  • Elgato Wave:3 ($130–150) — USB condenser with built-in (great) mixer software. Best for streaming.
  • Rode NT-USB+ ($150–170) — USB condenser with pop filter. Warm sound, good for voiceover and music.
  • Shure MV7 ($200–250) — Dynamic USB/XLR mic. Broadcast-quality, excellent noise rejection. The best all-rounder.
  • XLR Setup (Scarlett 2i2 + Shure SM58) ($250–350) — Pro-level dynamic mic. Requires interface. Best audio quality for the price.
USB Hubs & Docks Tips: For a clean desk setup, get a good Thunderbolt 4 dock (CalDigit TS4, Anker 777, Kensington SD5700T) for $200–350. Budget alternative: a USB-C hub with HDMI 2.1, 100W PD, and USB-A 3.0 for $30–60 (Anker, UGREEN, Baseus). Avoid no-name USB-C hubs — they can damage ports with poor power delivery.

Cables — Don't Skimp

Bad cables cause charging issues, data corruption, and even fires. Follow these guidelines:

  • USB-C: For laptops, get a 100W (5A) e-marked cable. For phones, 60W (3A) is fine. Anker, Belkin, Cable Matters, and UGREEN are reliable brands.
  • HDMI 2.1: Ultra High Speed certified for 4K 120 Hz or 8K 60 Hz. Avoid "HDMI 2.0" cables — HDMI 2.1 cables are backwards compatible and future-proof.
  • DisplayPort 1.4 / 2.0: Required for high-refresh-rate 4K/1440p monitors. Certified cables only from Accell, Club 3D, or Cable Matters.
  • Ethernet: Cat 6A is the sweet spot (10 Gbps, 100 m). Cat 8 is overkill for home use. Avoid flat cables — they are more prone to interference.

Monitor Arms

Monitor arms free up desk space, improve ergonomics, and look clean. Key specs: gas spring vs pneumatic (spring is better for heavier monitors), weight capacity (check your monitor weight + VESA adapter), and clamp vs grommet mount. Budget: Huanuo dual arm ($40–60). Mid: Amazon Basics / Ergotron LX ($100–150). High-end: Ergotron HX ($200–250, needed for ultrawides).
