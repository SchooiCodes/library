Scene Release Groups & Standards

The inner workings of the warez scene: release naming conventions, types, groups, P2P vs Scene, and how to find scene releases.

What is the Scene?

The “Scene” refers to a clandestine, organized community of groups that release pirated media (movies, TV, music, software, games) before anyone else. The Scene has strict rules about how releases are named, packaged, and distributed.

  • Not P2P: The Scene uses private FTP servers, not public torrents. Scene releases trickle down to the public via pre databases and torrent trackers.
  • Competition: Groups compete to be the first (“pre”) to release a given title.
  • Rules: Strict standards for encoding, NFO files, and packaging. Breaking rules gets a group banned.

Release Naming

Every Scene release follows a strict naming convention that encodes all metadata:

# Movie: Movie.Name.2024.1080p.BluRay.x264-GROUPNAME
# TV:    Show.Name.S01E05.1080p.WEB-DL.DDP5.1.H.264-GROUP
# Game:  Game.Name-REPACKNAME
# Music: Artist.Name-2024-Album-Name-FLAC-GROUP
#
# Key components:
# - Resolution: 720p, 1080p, 2160p (4K)
# - Source: BluRay, WEB-DL, WEBRip, HDTV, DVDRip
# - Codec: x264, x265, H.264, H.265, AV1
# - Audio: AAC, DTS, DDP5.1, FLAC, MP3
# - GROUP: The releasing group abbreviation

What is the Scene? (In Detail)

The “Scene” refers to a clandestine, organized community of groups that release pirated media (movies, TV, music, software, games) before anyone else. It operates like a competitive underground economy with strict rules, internal politics, and a culture of speed and quality.

  • Origins: The Scene began in the 1980s with software cracking groups (warez). It evolved to include movies, TV, music, and games. The structure and traditions are rooted in the early BBS (Bulletin Board System) era.
  • How it is organized: Groups are composed of members who handle different roles: suppliers (provide source materials), crackers/rippers (remove protection/encode), packagers (create the release), couriers (upload to topsites).
  • Topsites: Private FTP servers with massive storage and high-speed connections (10-100 Gbps). Releases are uploaded to topsites first, then trickle down to torrent trackers and DDL sites.
  • Racing: Groups compete to release a title first. The first to release a given title gets the "PRE" (prestige). Couriers compete to upload fastest to topsites. Racing is intense — seconds can matter.
  • Not P2P: The Scene uses private FTP servers, not public torrents. Scene releases trickle down to the public via pre databases and torrent trackers.

Release Groups vs P2P

Understanding the difference between Scene release groups and P2P (Peer-to-Peer) groups is crucial for evaluating quality:

Aspect Scene Groups P2P Groups
RulesStrict, standardized across all groupsLoose, group-dependent

Which is better? It depends. Scene releases are consistent and fast. P2P groups often produce higher-quality encodes with better source material. For archival purposes, many prefer P2P internal groups from private trackers.

Scene vs P2P Quality Differences

Beyond naming and rules, there are practical quality differences between Scene and P2P releases:

  • Bitrate: Scene releases use standardized bitrates that must fit within the Scene’s size rules. P2P groups can use higher bitrates if they choose, often resulting in better quality per file size.
  • Source material: Scene groups often encode from the first available source (even if imperfect). P2P groups may wait for better sources (e.g., wait for BluRay instead of encoding from a WEB-DL).
  • Audio: Scene releases often strip secondary audio tracks (commentaries, alternative dubs) to save space. P2P groups may include more audio options.
  • Encoding settings: Scene uses standardized encoder settings (x264/x265 presets). P2P groups may tune encoder settings for the specific content, achieving better compression quality.
  • HDR/DV: P2P groups are generally better at handling HDR10+ and Dolby Vision metadata. Scene releases sometimes strip or improperly encode HDR metadata.

Recommendation: For mainstream movies and TV, Scene releases are excellent and convenient. For your favorite films or archival quality, seek out P2P internal group releases from private trackers.

Scene Standards

The Scene has detailed standards documents (sometimes called "rules" or "standards") that govern how releases must be packaged:

  • Packaging: Releases must be in RAR format (split into numbered parts). Each part is typically 50 MB (though larger parts are allowed for modern releases). The RARs must be recoverable (at least 10% recovery records).
  • NFO files: Every release must include a properly formatted NFO file (ASCII art + metadata). See below for the NFO structure.
  • SFV files: A Simple File Verification (.sfv) file must be included with CRC32 checksums for each RAR. See below.
  • Sample: Most video releases require a sample file (typically 1-2 minutes, 100 MB) to preview the quality.
  • Proof: Some categories require proof images (screenshots from the source showing the release is genuine).
  • No re-encoding: Scene rules strictly forbid releasing a re-encode of another Scene release. You must encode from the original source.

Topsites and Racing

Topsites are the backbone of Scene distribution:

  • What is a topsite: A private FTP server with massive storage (100+ TB), high-speed connectivity (10-100 Gbps), and strict user management. Access is invitation-only and fiercely protected.
  • Racing: Couriers compete to upload releases to topsites as fast as possible. The first to upload a release to a topsite gets "credit" which is used to maintain their access and status.
  • Race bots: Automated scripts (bots) monitor IRC announce channels for new releases. When a release is pre’d, bots instantly initiate downloads to their own topsites. The race to be the first to download is as competitive as the race to release.
  • Spread: From the first topsite, a release spreads to other topsites via "spread" bots that automatically transfer between trusted sites. Eventually, the release makes its way to torrent trackers and the public internet (via "leakers").
  • Leaking: Someone with topsite access copies a release and uploads it to a public tracker or DDL site. This is how Scene content reaches the general public. Many private trackers have strict rules against leaking Scene content from internal sources.

Pre Database Sites

Pre databases (PreDBs) track Scene releases as they happen. They are the best way to see what has been released recently:

  • predb.me: The most popular PreDB. Aggregates releases from multiple sources. Search by release name, group, category, or date. Clean interface, RSS feeds, and API available.
  • predb.ovh: Alternative PreDB with a modern interface. Supports regex search and filtering by category.
  • r/CrackWatch: Reddit community that tracks game releases. Posts new game cracks and Scene releases as they happen. Community discusses release quality.
  • PreDB IRC channels: Many PreDBs have IRC announce channels where releases are announced in real-time. Faster than website monitoring.
  • Using PreDBs: Search for a specific release to see if it has been pre’d. Monitor for new releases to stay up to date with the Scene. PreDBs are also useful for verifying release names (matching exact Scene naming).

NFO File Structure

NFO files are ASCII art text files that accompany every Scene release. They follow a standard structure:

+-----[ RELEASE NAME ]-----------------------------------+
|                                                          |
| Title:       Movie.Name.2024.1080p.BluRay.x264-GROUPNAME |
| Supplier:    supplier_nick                               |
| Ripper:      ripper_nick                                 |
| Packager:    packager_nick                               |
|                                                          |
| Source:      BluRay                                      |
| Resolution:  1920x1080                                   |
| Codec:       x264 (crf 18, preset slow)                 |
| Audio:       English DTS-HD MA 5.1 + Commentary          |
| Subtitles:   English, Spanish, French                    |
| Runtime:     2h 28m                                      |
| Size:        15.3 GB / 30x50MB RARs                      |
|                                                          |
| Note:        First scene release of this title           |
|              Encoded from retail Blu-ray disc            |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
| [ GROUP NAME PRESENTS ]                                  |
| ASCII art of group logo follows here...                  |
+----------------------------------------------------------+

File Verification (SFV)

SFV (Simple File Verification) files ensure data integrity for Scene releases:

  • What it contains: A plain text file listing every RAR file in the release and its CRC32 checksum. Example: movie-name.part01.rar 2A1B3C4D
  • How to use: Download the entire release (all RARs + SFV). Run an SFV checker (e.g., RapidCRC, QuickSFV) against the SFV file. It verifies each RAR’s checksum matches.
  • Why it matters: If any file is corrupted during download, the SFV check will identify which part needs to be re-downloaded. This is essential for Usenet (where DMCA may hit individual parts) and for large torrent packs.
  • Automated verification: SABnzbd, NZBGet, and most torrent clients have built-in SFV verification. They automatically check and repair files during the download process.

Scene Rules

The Scene operates under a strict set of rules (Version 6.x+ for most categories). Breaking these rules leads to the group being "nuked" (banned/named in release):

  • No dupes: A group cannot release a title that has already been released by another group (unless encoding from a better source).
  • Proper packaging: RAR format required. No self-extracting archives (SFX). Recovery records mandatory (at least 10%).
  • No re-encodes: Must encode from original source, not from another Scene release. Encode quality must meet minimum standards.
  • Naming rules: Strict naming conventions (see above). Spaces become dots. No special characters except dashes and dots.
  • Category specific:
    • Movies: Minimum resolution 720p for HD releases. Correct aspect ratio required. No watermarks or subtitles burned into video.
    • Music: FLAC for lossless, MP3 at minimum 320kbps CBR for lossy. Proper tagging and cue sheets required.
    • Games: No modification of game executables beyond what is necessary for the crack. Proper installer required.
    • Software: Must be the full retail version, not a trial or demo.
  • Nuke reasons: Releases can be "nuked" (invalidated) for various reasons: bad encoding, incorrect naming, fake/watermarked source, missing files, virus/malware in release.

How to Identify Scene Releases

Even on public trackers, you can identify Scene releases by their naming and packaging:

  • Naming: Scene releases always follow the strict naming convention: Title.Year.Source.Quality.Codec-GROUP. If you see dots between words and a group name at the end (after a dash), it is likely a Scene release.
  • RAR archives: Scene releases are always in RAR format (multiple .rar files). P2P releases are often in MKV or MP4 containers.
  • NFO files: Scene releases always include a .nfo file with ASCII art and metadata. P2P releases may or may not include one.
  • SFV files: Scene releases include a .sfv file. P2P releases typically do not.
  • Sample files: Scene video releases usually include a sample file. P2P releases rarely do.
  • Group names: Familiarize yourself with active Scene groups (SPARTA, ViP3R, FGT, KOGi, ION10, etc.). When you see these groups, it is a Scene release.

Scene Release Etiquette

If you become involved in the Scene (as a member or courier), these etiquette rules apply:

  • No leaking: Never leak Scene releases to public trackers before the group allows it. Leaking is the fastest way to get banned from the Scene.
  • Proper credit: Always credit the releasing group. Do not modify NFO files or rename releases.
  • Do not re-encode Scene releases: Encoding from a Scene release and releasing it yourself is against Scene rules. You must encode from the original source.
  • Respect topsite rules: If you have topsite access, do not share your account. Do not download more than allowed. Do not use FTP accelerators that abuse the server.
  • Racing etiquette: Do not race against your own group members. Coordinate uploads to avoid flooding topsites with duplicate content.
  • Help each other: The Scene is a community. Help other groups with source material, encoding tips, and technical issues. The stronger the Scene, the better for everyone.
