What is Usenet?

Usenet is a decentralized discussion and file distribution system that predates the World Wide Web. Unlike torrenting’s P2P model, Usenet uses a client-server architecture: you download files directly from news servers that store the data.

  • How it works: Files are split into posts, encoded into NZB files (like .torrent files), and uploaded to news servers. Your NZB client downloads all the pieces from the server and reassembles them.
  • No seeding required: Unlike torrents, you don’t need to upload anything back. Download speeds are typically maxed out on your connection.
  • Retention: Good providers keep data for 10-15 years. Older content may be taken down via DMCA.
  • Cost: Usenet is not free. You pay for a provider (server access) and optional indexers (search engines).

What is Usenet? (In Detail)

Usenet is a decentralized discussion and file distribution system that predates the World Wide Web. Unlike torrenting’s P2P model, Usenet uses a client-server architecture: you download files directly from news servers that store the data.

How Usenet works for pirated content:

  • Content is uploaded to Usenet as individual posts (messages), typically split into small chunks (often 10-50 MB each).
  • These posts are encoded as text (yEnc encoding) and sent to specific newsgroups (e.g., alt.binaries.movies).
  • An NZB file acts like a torrent file — it contains all the metadata (message IDs, poster info, size) needed to locate and reassemble the content.
  • Your NZB client (SABnzbd, NZBGet) connects to your provider’s news server, downloads all the pieces, decodes them (yEnc), and reassembles the original file.
  • No seeding required: Unlike torrents, you don’t need to upload anything back. Download speeds are typically maxed out on your connection.
Key differences from torrenting: Usenet is not P2P. You download from a central server, not from peers. Your IP is never exposed to other users. No ratio, no seeding, no DHT. This makes Usenet inherently more private than torrenting.

Provider Tiers and Backbone Map

Usenet providers are not all equal. They belong to different backbones (networks) with different retention and completion rates:

  • Tier 1 (Omicron): The largest backbone. Owns Newshosting, Eweka, Giganews, TweakNews, Astraweb. Best retention (15+ years) and highest completion. Most expensive.
  • Tier 2 (Highwinds): Owns UsenetServer, NewsHosting, SuperNews. Good retention (10+ years), competitive pricing. Good for backup.
  • Tier 3 (Independent): Providers like UsenetExpress, BlockNews, ViperNews. Shorter retention (3-5 years) but cheaper. Often used as block account backups.

Backbone strategy: DMCA takedowns are per-backbone, not per-provider. If you and your provider share the same backbone (e.g., both on Omicron), content taken down from Omicron is gone for both. Best practice: Get one unlimited account on Omicron (primary) and one block account on a different backbone (backup). This gives you the highest chance of completing downloads.

Retention Explained

Retention is how far back a provider stores Usenet posts. Measured in days or years:

  • What affects retention: Storage is expensive. Older content takes up space without being downloaded much. Providers balance retention with cost.
  • Current retention limits:
    • Omicron (Eweka): ~5,500 days (15+ years)
    • Highwinds (Newshosting): ~4,500 days (12+ years)
    • UsenetExpress: ~3,000 days (8+ years)
  • Why retention matters: Older content gets DMCA’d faster than new content. Higher retention means older posts are still available. For popular older content, high retention is essential.
  • DMCA vs NTD: European providers (Eweka) follow local copyright laws which are more lenient than US DMCA. They take longer to process takedowns, meaning content stays available longer. Eweka is often recommended as a primary provider for this reason.

SABnzbd Setup Guide

SABnzbd is the most popular Usenet download client. Here is how to set it up properly:

  1. Install SABnzbd: Use Docker (linuxserver/sabnzbd), or download from sabnzbd.org for Windows/macOS/Linux.
  2. Configure servers: Go to Config → Servers. Add your Usenet provider:
    • Host: news.yourprovider.com
    • Port: 563 (SSL) or 119 (plain)
    • Username/Password: Your provider account credentials
    • Connections: 20-50 (your provider will specify max)
    • SSL: Enable for all connections
  3. Configure categories: Config → Categories. Set up tv, movies, music, books, software. Each category has its own folder and post-processing options.
  4. Configure post-processing: SABnzbd can automatically unpack RAR archives, repair with PAR2, delete the archive files, and sort into media folders.
  5. Security: Set a strong API key (Config → General → API Key). Enable HTTPS for remote access.
# SABnzbd Docker Compose:
sabnzbd:
  image: linuxserver/sabnzbd:latest
  container_name: sabnzbd
  ports:
    - "8080:8080"
  volumes:
    - ./sabnzbd-config:/config
    - /media/downloads:/downloads
    - /media/incomplete:/incomplete-downloads
  environment:
    - PUID=1000
    - PGID=1000
  restart: unless-stopped

Automation with Sonarr/Radarr + SABnzbd

The true power of Usenet is revealed when combined with automation tools. Here is the complete workflow:

  1. Prowlarr connects to your indexers (NZBGeek, DrunkenSlug, etc.) and syncs them to all your Arr apps.
  2. Sonarr/Radarr monitors your watchlist (shows, movies). When new content is available, it searches indexers via Prowlarr.
  3. Sonarr/Radarr sends the NZB to SABnzbd for downloading.
  4. SABnzbd downloads, repairs (PAR2), unpacks, and moves the files to a completed folder.
  5. Sonarr/Radarr picks up the completed files, renames them according to your naming scheme, and moves them to your media folders.
  6. Plex/Jellyfin monitors your media folders, detects new files, and scrapes metadata automatically.

Block Accounts vs Unlimited

Understanding the difference between block accounts and unlimited accounts helps you optimize cost:

Type Cost Model Best For
UnlimitedMonthly/yearly subscriptionHeavy downloaders, daily users
Block accountOne-time payment for X GBBackup provider, light users

Strategy: Get one unlimited account (e.g., Eweka at ~$6/mo) as your primary. Get one block account on a different backbone (e.g., UsenetExpress 1 TB block for ~$35, lasts years) as backup. When your primary misses a download (DMCA), the backup fills in.

SSL Connections

Always use SSL (port 563) or TLS (port 563) when connecting to your Usenet provider:

  • Why: Without encryption, your ISP can see every file you download via deep packet inspection. With SSL, they only see encrypted traffic to your provider.
  • Setup: In SABnzbd/NZBGet, set the port to 563 and enable "SSL/TLS." Some providers require a separate SSL port (check your provider’s documentation).
  • Certificate validation: Enable certificate validation to prevent man-in-the-middle attacks. SABnzbd and NZBGet handle this automatically.
  • Performance impact: Minimal (<5% overhead). Modern CPUs handle SSL encryption without noticeable slowdown.

Completion vs Failure

Completion refers to how much of an NZB post is still available on your provider’s servers:

  • 100% completion: All pieces of the post are available. Download proceeds normally.
  • Partial completion: Some pieces are missing (DMCA takedown). Your client uses PAR2 parity files to reconstruct missing pieces. If enough PAR2 blocks exist, the download succeeds.
  • Failed download: Too many pieces missing, insufficient PAR2 blocks. The download fails. SABnzbd marks it as failed and can try an alternative NZB if configured.
  • Improving completion:
    • Use a provider on a different backbone as backup (DMCA redundancy).
    • Use multiple indexers (one may have a different NZB version with better completion).
    • Download within hours of a post going up (before DMCA hits).

PAR2 Repair

PAR2 (Parity Archive Volume) files are essential for Usenet reliability:

  • How it works: When a file is uploaded to Usenet, the uploader also uploads PAR2 parity blocks. These blocks contain mathematical checksums of the file data. If some pieces are missing (DMCA), the parity blocks can reconstruct them.
  • PAR2 percentage: A 10% PAR2 set means the uploader included 10% extra parity data. If 10% of the post is missing, the file can still be fully reconstructed.
  • In action: SABnzbd automatically detects missing pieces and runs PAR2 repair. You can see this in the queue status: "Repairing..." with a progress bar. This is normal and happens frequently.
  • Failed repair: If insufficient PAR2 blocks exist, the download fails. SABnzbd marks it as failed. You can try to find an alternative NZB of the same content.

Obfuscated Posts

To evade automated DMCA takedowns, uploaders use obfuscation techniques:

  • Random filenames: Instead of Movie.Name.2024.1080p.mkv, files are named aB3xK9mN.01. SABnzbd/NZBGet can rename on completion.
  • Encrypted archives: Some uploads are RAR archives with random passwords. The password is included in the NZB metadata or in the post description.
  • How indexers help: Indexers decode obfuscated posts, extract metadata, and present them with proper names in their search results. Good indexers handle most obfuscation automatically.
  • Impact on users: None, if using a good indexer. SABnzbd/NZBGet handle renaming and extraction automatically.
