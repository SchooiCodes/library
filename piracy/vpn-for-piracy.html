Why You Need a VPN

Your real IP address is visible to everyone in a torrent swarm. ISPs monitor for pirating activity and copyright trolls track IPs to send DMCA notices. A VPN hides your IP by routing traffic through an encrypted tunnel to a remote server.

VPN Features for Piracy

  • No logging policy: VPN must not keep logs of your activity.
  • Port forwarding: Essential for good torrent speeds. Not all VPNs offer this.
  • Kill switch: Blocks all internet traffic if VPN connection drops.
  • Split tunneling: Route only torrent traffic through VPN, use normal connection for everything else.
  • WireGuard protocol: Modern, fast, lightweight. OpenVPN is also good.

Port Forwarding

Port forwarding allows incoming connections to your torrent client. Without it, you can only connect to peers that have open ports. This severely limits your upload speed and ratio building. Mullvad, AirVPN, ProtonVPN, and OVPN support port forwarding.

Kill Switch

A kill switch blocks all internet traffic if the VPN disconnects unexpectedly. This is your last line of defense against IP leaks. Most VPN clients have this built-in. Enable it in your VPN settings.

Port Forwarding Explained

Port forwarding is essential for efficient BitTorrent usage. Here is why it matters and how it works:

  • Without port forwarding: You can only connect to peers that have open ports (leechers who also lack port forwarding). Many peers are unreachable, severely limiting your available connections and upload speed.
  • With port forwarding: Peers can initiate connections to you. You become reachable to more peers, which drastically improves upload speeds, download speeds on older/rare torrents, and ratio building on private trackers.
  • How to check: Visit canyouseeme.org or use the port check tool in qBittorrent (Tools → Options → Connection → Test port).

Recommendation: Use WireGuard whenever available. It is faster, more modern, and uses fewer resources. Only use OpenVPN if your VPN provider doesn’t support WireGuard or you need specific OpenVPN features (like TCP on port 443 for restrictive networks).

Kill Switch Testing Methodology

Never trust a VPN's kill switch without testing it. Here is how to verify it works:

  1. Go to ipleak.net or ipleak.org and note your real IP address.
  2. Connect to your VPN and confirm the IP changed to the VPN server.
  3. Force the VPN to disconnect:
    • On desktop: End the VPN process in Task Manager or Activity Monitor.
    • On mobile: Enable airplane mode briefly.
    • For thorough testing: Unplug your router or disable your network interface.
  4. Immediately check ipleak.net again. If the site loads or shows your real IP, the kill switch has failed.
  5. Test with mullvad.net/check if using Mullvad — it shows detailed connection info and DNS leak status.
Critical: Some VPN kill switches only block new connections after a disconnect. Existing connections (like an active torrent transfer) may continue briefly without protection. Use the bind to interface feature in your torrent client as an additional safety measure.

DNS Leak Prevention

A DNS leak occurs when your device sends DNS queries outside the VPN tunnel, revealing your browsing activity and real IP to your ISP:

  • Why leaks happen: Some VPN apps don't redirect DNS traffic by default. Windows/macOS may use system DNS servers instead of the VPN's DNS.
  • Check for leaks: Visit dnsleaktest.com or ipleak.net while connected. If you see your ISP's DNS servers, you have a leak.
  • Fix leaks: Set custom DNS servers in your VPN client settings (e.g., Mullvad DNS: 194.242.2.2, 194.242.2.3). Disable IPv6 if your VPN doesn't support it. Use netsh interface ipv6 set state disabled on Windows.
  • DNS providers for privacy: Quad9 (9.9.9.9), Cloudflare (1.1.1.1), AdGuard DNS (94.140.14.14).
# Windows: Force DNS through VPN interface
# Set DNS server on VPN adapter:
netsh interface ip set dns name="Your VPN Interface" static 10.0.0.1

# Prevent Microsoft DNS leak:
netsh interface ip delete dnsserver "Your VPN Interface" all
ipconfig /flushdns

Split Tunneling

Split tunneling lets you route only specific traffic through the VPN while everything else uses your normal connection. Useful for keeping your torrent traffic private while accessing local network devices or region-locked services normally.

  • App-based split tunneling: Route only specific applications (e.g., qBittorrent) through the VPN. Available in Mullvad, ProtonVPN, and AirVPN clients.
  • IP-based split tunneling: Route only traffic to specific IP ranges or ports through the VPN. More granular but harder to configure.
  • Use case: Keep Plex/Jellyfin accessible on your local network while torrent traffic goes through the VPN.
  • Use case: Access banking websites (which often block VPN IPs) while torrenting through the VPN.
Warning: Split tunneling can accidentally leak your real IP if misconfigured. Always test with ipleak.net after setup. For maximum safety, route all traffic through the VPN and accept the slight inconvenience.

VPN for Seedboxes

Seedboxes are remote servers that torrent for you. They often have their own IP and don't need a VPN. However:

  • Public tracker seedboxes: Some seedbox providers require a VPN if you're torrenting on public trackers (DMCA concerns). Others provide DMCA-ignored connections.
  • Private tracker seedboxes: Generally don't need a VPN since the tracker community handles DMCA in-house.
  • Self-hosted VPN on seedbox: Install WireGuard on your seedbox and VPN into it from your home machine. This encrypts the FTP/SFTP transfer from seedbox to home.
  • Provider-level VPN: Some seedboxes offer a bundled VPN (e.g., UltraSeedbox, Whatbox).

No-Log Audits Explained

A "no-log" policy is only trustworthy if independently audited. Here is what to look for:

  • Third-party audits: Reputable VPNs hire security firms (Cure53, PwC, Bishop Fox) to audit their infrastructure. Mullvad and IVPN publish full audit reports online.
  • Court cases: PIA has proven their no-log policy in court multiple times (they couldn't provide user data when subpoenaed). This is stronger than any audit.
  • Technical implementation: Look for RAM-only servers (no hard drives) where logs disappear on reboot. Mullvad and IVPN use RAM-based servers exclusively.
  • What to avoid: VPNs that claim "no logs" but have never been audited, have a history of logging, or are based in 14-Eyes countries without a clear legal track record.

Multi-Hop / VPN Chaining

Multi-hop routes your traffic through two or more VPN servers in different jurisdictions. This adds a layer of protection: even if one VPN is compromised, the other still protects you:

  • How it works: Your traffic is encrypted by the first VPN server, decrypted, re-encrypted by the second server, then sent to the destination. Neither server sees both your real IP and the destination.
  • Providers supporting multi-hop: Mullvad (via WireGuard + SOCKS5), IVPN (built-in multi-hop), ProtonVPN (Secure Core).
  • Performance impact: Expect 20-40% speed reduction due to double encryption/hop latency. Worth it if you prioritize maximum anonymity over speed.
  • For torrenting: Multi-hop is generally not needed. A single VPN connection with port forwarding is sufficient for most users. Multi-hop breaks port forwarding.
