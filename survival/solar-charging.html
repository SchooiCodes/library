Solar & Off-Grid Power

Keep devices running off-grid - solar panel basics, power station sizing, battery types, USB power banks, inverters, and practical tips for extended outages.

Solar Panel Basics

Key specs: Watts (power output), Voltage (12V standard for small systems). Monocrystalline (18-22% efficient) vs Polycrystalline (15-18%).

Sizing: 20-50W for phone charging, 100-200W for laptop + devices, 300-600W for fridge + lights, 1000W+ for whole house.

Charge controller: MPPT (20-30% more efficient) over PWM for serious setups.

Power Station Sizing

Step 1: List device power needs (watts x hours = Wh/day).

Step 2: Add 50% margin. Step 3: Choose station >= that Wh.

Popular: Jackery, Bluetti AC200P (~2000 Wh), EcoFlow Delta 2, Anker PowerHouse. Or DIY (battery + inverter + MPPT).

USB Power Banks

Sizes: 5000 mAh (1 phone, keychain), 10000 mAh (2-3, daily carry), 20000 mAh (4-6, weekend), 50000 mAh (12-15, week+).

Features: USB-C PD, 18W+ output, pass-through charging, LED display, multiple ports.

Solar power banks: Built-in panels are too small (1-2W). Better: 20-50W foldable panel + regular power bank.

Brands: Anker, RavPower, Aukey, Xiaomi, Nitecore.

DC to AC Inverters

Types: Modified sine wave (cheaper, buzz in audio) vs Pure sine wave (cleaner, works with everything). Always choose PSW for emergency prep.

Sizing: Add all device watts + 20% headroom. 100-300W for small devices, 500-1000W with fridge, 2000W+ for heavy loads.

Safety: Never exceed rating, ensure ventilation, use thick cables, install fuse.

Keeping Devices Alive

Phone: Battery saver, minimum brightness, airplane mode when not communicating, charge at 30% (better for battery health).

Laptop: Dim screen, power saver, disconnect peripherals, close browser tabs, hibernate instead of sleep.

Priority: Communication devices > medical > work > lighting > entertainment.

Start small: 20W solar + 20000 mAh power bank + USB cable. ~$80. Keeps your phone running indefinitely off-grid.

Portable vs Fixed Panels

Portable panels (20-200W) fold or roll up for transport. Ideal for camping, emergency kits, and temporary setups. Sacrifice some efficiency for convenience. Brands: Anker, BigBlue, Goal Zero, Renogy.

Fixed panels (100-400W) are mounted on rooftops or ground mounts. Maximum efficiency, best for permanent installations. Require mounting hardware and proper orientation. Brands: Renogy, Canadian Solar, LG, SunPower.

Power Calculation for Device Charging

Sizing a solar system requires calculating your daily power needs and matching them to solar panel output. Here is the step-by-step method:

# Step 1: Calculate daily power needs (Watt-hours)
# Device watts × hours of use = Wh/day
Phone:       5W × 2h = 10 Wh/day
Laptop:     60W × 3h = 180 Wh/day
Tablet:     10W × 4h = 40 Wh/day
Router:      10W × 24h = 240 Wh/day
LED lights: 5W × 6h = 30 Wh/day
Total:               = 500 Wh/day

# Step 2: Add 50% margin for inefficiency
500 Wh × 1.5 = 750 Wh/day needed

# Step 3: Calculate solar panel size
# Average peak sun hours by region:
# Desert (AZ, NV): ~6 hours
# Temperate (US East): ~4.5 hours
# Northern (Canada, UK): ~3 hours
# Cloudy season: ~2 hours

# Panel size = Daily Wh needed / Peak sun hours
# For 750 Wh in temperate climate (4.5h):
750 / 4.5 = 167W minimum panel size
# Round up: 200W panel recommended
Key formulas: Watts = Volts × Amps. Watt-hours = Watts × Hours. A 100W panel at 12V produces 8.3A. In 5 hours of peak sun, it generates 500 Wh — enough to charge a phone 50 times or a laptop 3-4 times.

Battery Storage Deep Dive

Batteries store the energy your solar panels produce for use when the sun is not shining. Choosing the right battery type and capacity is critical for a reliable off-grid system.

Power Bank vs Deep Cycle Battery

  • Power banks (10,000-50,000 mAh) — Best for charging phones, tablets, and small devices. Built-in USB ports make them simple to use. Capacity is measured in mAh at 3.7V. A 20,000 mAh power bank stores ~74 Wh. Can charge a phone 3-5 times.
  • Deep cycle batteries (50-200 Ah) — Designed for sustained power delivery over hours. Used in larger solar systems with inverters. Measure capacity in Amp-hours at 12V. A 100 Ah battery stores 1,200 Wh — enough to run a fridge for 10+ hours.

Lithium vs Lead-Acid

  • LiFePO4 (Lithium Iron Phosphate) — Best overall choice. 10-15 year lifespan, 80-90% usable capacity, lightweight, no maintenance. 3-5x the cost of lead-acid but lower cost per cycle over lifetime.
  • Lithium NMC — Higher energy density than LiFePO4. Used in power banks and portable power stations. 5-8 year lifespan. Slightly less safe than LiFePO4 (thermal runaway risk if damaged).
  • AGM (Absorbent Glass Mat) Lead-Acid — Budget choice. 3-5 year lifespan, 50% usable capacity (never discharge below 50%), heavy, requires ventilation. Good for stationary backup where weight is not a concern.

Capacity Calculation

# Battery capacity needed for 1 day of autonomy:
# Daily needs: 500 Wh
# System voltage: 12V
# Battery type: LiFePO4 (80% usable)

Battery Wh needed = 500 Wh / 0.8 = 625 Wh
Battery Ah needed = 625 Wh / 12V = 52 Ah
Round up: 60 Ah LiFePO4 battery

# For 3 days of autonomy (cloudy weather):
60 Ah × 3 = 180 Ah LiFePO4 battery
# Or use a 200 Ah battery for safety margin

Charge Controllers: PWM vs MPPT

The charge controller regulates the voltage and current from your solar panels to safely charge your battery. Choosing between PWM and MPPT has a significant impact on system efficiency.

PWM (Pulse Width Modulation)

PWM controllers are simpler and cheaper ($10-50). They work by connecting the panel directly to the battery and pulsing the connection to regulate voltage. Panel voltage is pulled down to battery voltage, wasting excess voltage.

Best for: Small systems (<100W) where panel voltage is close to battery voltage (e.g., 18V panel → 12V battery). Efficiency: ~70-80%.

MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracking)

MPPT controllers are more advanced and expensive ($30-200). They electronically track the panel's maximum power point and convert excess voltage to additional current. In cold weather, a 12V panel can produce 20V — MPPT converts that extra voltage into 30% more charging current.

Best for: Systems over 100W, cold climates, or when panel voltage is significantly higher than battery voltage (e.g., 36V panel → 12V battery). Efficiency: ~90-97%.

# Charge controller sizing:
# Total panel wattage / Battery voltage × Safety factor
# Example: 200W panel / 12V battery × 1.25 = 20.8A
# Use a 30A MPPT charge controller

# PWM example: 100W panel / 12V battery = 8.3A
# Use a 10A PWM controller (cheap, fine for small systems)
Recommendation: For any emergency solar setup over 50W, spend the extra money on an MPPT controller. The 20-30% efficiency gain means you get more power from the same panel — critical when every watt counts.

Device Charging Efficiency

Understanding charging efficiency helps you get the most out of your limited solar power. Here are the key factors affecting how much power actually reaches your devices:

  • USB conversion loss — Converting 12V battery power to 5V USB loses ~15-20%. Using a quality USB adapter (Anker, RavPower) minimizes this loss.
  • Inverter loss — Converting DC battery power to AC (for laptop chargers) loses ~10-20%. Avoid inverters when possible — charge laptops directly via USB-C PD or 12V car chargers.
  • Cable resistance — Thin, long USB cables lose power. Use short (3ft/1m), thick (20 AWG or lower) cables. USB-C cables should be rated for 60W or 100W.
  • Battery charging curves — Li-ion batteries charge fastest between 20-80%. The last 20% charges slowest (trickle charge). Charge phones to 80% to maximize solar efficiency.
  • Solar panel angle — A panel pointed directly at the sun produces maximum power. Off-angle reduces output by 20-50%. Adjust angle every 2-3 hours for best results.

Solar for Laptops vs Phones vs Tablets

Different devices have very different power requirements. Plan your solar system around your highest-priority device:

  • Phone (~5-10W charge rate) — Needs only 10-20 Wh per full charge. A 20W panel + 10,000 mAh power bank can keep a phone running indefinitely. Absolute minimum setup: $60.
  • Tablet (~10-20W charge rate) — Needs 20-40 Wh per full charge. A 28W panel + 20,000 mAh power bank provides comfortable charging. Minimum: $100.
  • Laptop (~45-100W charge rate) — Needs 40-100 Wh per full charge. Requires a 60-100W panel or a power station. USB-C PD laptops (MacBook, Dell XPS) can charge directly from a power bank. Older laptops need an inverter. Minimum: $200.
  • Multiple devices — A 100W panel + 50Ah battery + MPPT controller covers phone + tablet + laptop + router + lights. This is the recommended minimum for a household emergency setup.

Weather Considerations

Solar panel output varies dramatically with weather conditions. Plan for worst-case scenarios:

  • Full sun — 100% rated output. Panel produces full wattage.
  • Light clouds — 50-80% of rated output. Still usable for charging.
  • Heavy clouds — 10-30% of rated output. Only enough for trickle charging. A 100W panel produces only 10-30W.
  • Rain — 5-15% of rated output. Panels still produce some power but not enough for direct charging. Best used to charge a battery for later use.
  • Snow coverage — 0% output if covered. Snow slides off tilted panels. Brush snow off ground-mounted or portable panels.
  • Temperature — Panels lose ~0.4% efficiency per degree C above 25C. On a 40C day, a 100W panel produces only ~94W. Cold weather (below 25C) actually improves efficiency.

Strategy: Size your system for cloudy days, not sunny ones. If you need 500 Wh/day, assume you will get only 2 peak sun hours on cloudy days. You need 250W of panels to meet your needs on bad weather days.

Angle and Positioning for Maximum Capture

Proper panel orientation can increase daily energy harvest by 30-50% compared to a poorly positioned panel.

  • Direction — In the northern hemisphere, face panels true south. In the southern hemisphere, face true north. Within 15 degrees of the correct direction is acceptable.
  • Tilt angle — Set the panel tilt equal to your latitude for year-round average. Adjust seasonally: latitude + 15 degrees in winter, latitude - 15 degrees in summer.
  • Seasonal chart: New York (40.7N): Winter tilt = 56°, Summer tilt = 26°, Spring/Fall = 40°. Phoenix (33.4N): Winter = 48°, Summer = 18°, Spring/Fall = 33°.
  • No adjustments? — Set at latitude angle and leave it. You will lose 10-15% efficiency compared to seasonal adjustment, but it is better than nothing.
  • Portable panels — Lay flat on the ground for 80% efficiency when sun is high. Prop up at an angle for better early morning and late afternoon collection. Portable panels with adjustable kickstands are best.

Cable Management and Connectors

Proper cables and connectors are essential for safety and efficiency. Undersized wires cause voltage drop, heat buildup, and fire risk.

  • MC4 connectors — Standard solar panel connectors. Waterproof, tool-less disconnect. Used on all modern solar panels.
  • Wire gauge guide: For runs under 10 feet: 10 AWG for 20A, 8 AWG for 30A, 6 AWG for 50A. For longer runs, step up one gauge size to prevent voltage drop (keep drop under 3%).
  • Anderson Powerpole — Standard connector for portable solar and battery connections. Used by many emergency communication systems. Easy to assemble with crimp tools.
  • USB cables — Use USB-C for everything modern (phones, tablets, laptops). Carry spares — USB cables are the most likely component to fail. A 100W USB-C cable supports laptop charging.
  • Battery terminals — Use ring terminals for permanent connections. Never use alligator clips for high-current connections (they can overheat). For temporary setups, Anderson Powerpole connectors are ideal.

Maintaining Battery Health

Proper battery maintenance extends battery life significantly and ensures your system works when you need it:

  • LiFePO4: Store at 50% charge. Charge between 0-45C (use below 0C can damage). Do not leave at 100% for extended periods. Cycle at least once every 3 months.
  • Lead-acid (AGM): Keep at 100% charge (float voltage). Never discharge below 50%. Equalize charge monthly (14.4-14.8V for 2-4 hours). Replace every 3-5 years.
  • Power banks (Li-ion): Store at 50-70% charge in a cool place. Charge and discharge at least once every 6 months. Replace every 2-4 years. Heat is the #1 killer — never leave in a car in summer.
  • Temperature effects: Battery capacity drops in cold: LiFePO4 loses ~20% at 0C, ~40% at -10C. Lead-acid loses ~30% at 0C, ~50% at -10C. Keep batteries above freezing for best performance.

DIY Solar Charging Station

Building your own solar charging station from components is more work than buying a pre-built kit, but it is significantly cheaper and more customizable.

# DIY solar charging station components:
# Parts list:
# - 100W monocrystalline solar panel: $80-120
# - 30A MPPT charge controller: $40-80
# - 12V 50Ah LiFePO4 battery: $150-250
# - 300W pure sine wave inverter: $40-80
# - 10 AWG solar cable + MC4 connectors: $20
# - 12V USB charging panel: $10-15
# - Fuse holder + 30A fuse: $10
# - Weatherproof enclosure (Pelican case or tool box): $30-50
# Total: ~$380-625 compared to $1000+ for a pre-built kit

# Assembly steps:
# 1. Mount charge controller and fuse in enclosure
# 2. Connect battery to charge controller (battery terminals first)
# 3. Connect solar panel to charge controller (PV terminals)
# 4. Connect inverter to battery (via fuse)
# 5. Connect USB panel to battery
# 6. Test with a small device before relying on it

# Safety:
# - Always fuse the battery connection (30A between battery and controller)
# - Use proper gauge wire (10 AWG for up to 30A, 8 AWG for up to 50A)
# - Ventilate the enclosure if using lead-acid batteries
# - Never short circuit battery terminals
Start small: Before building a full system, start with a 20W solar panel and a 20,000 mAh power bank. This ~$80 setup charges phones indefinitely. If you find yourself needing more power, then invest in a larger system. Most people overestimate their power needs — the small setup covers 90% of emergency charging scenarios.
