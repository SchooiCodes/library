Online Safety & Digital Hygiene

Overview

Online safety is not about any single tool — it is about building habits. Strong passwords, recognizing scams, keeping devices updated, and understanding your digital footprint all work together to reduce risk. This guide covers the everyday practices that make the biggest difference in protecting yourself online.

Password Hygiene

Weak or reused passwords are the #1 cause of account compromise. A password manager is essential.

Recommended Password Managers

Manager Open Source Platform Key Feature
BitwardenYesAll platformsSelf-hostable, $10/yr premium

Proton PassYesAll platformsE2EE, integrated with Proton

Password Rules

  • Length > complexity: 16+ characters is more important than symbols
  • Never reuse passwords across different services
  • Use passphrases: correct-horse-battery-staple is easier to type and remember
  • Enable 2FA on your password manager itself
# Generate a strong password with Bitwarden CLI
bw generate --length 20 --uppercase --lowercase --numbers --special

# Generate with openssl (Unix)
openssl rand -base64 24

Browser Security

Your browser is the gateway to the internet. Lock it down.

Essential Extensions

Extension Purpose Open Source
uBlock OriginAd/tracker blocking, malware domain filteringYes
HTTPS EverywhereEnforce HTTPS connectionsYes
Privacy BadgerBlocks invisible trackersYes
BitwardenPassword autofill with phishing detectionYes
NoScriptJavaScript whitelist controlYes

Browser Settings

  • Disable third-party cookies: Chrome: Settings → Privacy → Block third-party cookies
  • Enable "Do Not Track" — not always honored but costs nothing
  • Clear cookies and cache regularly, or use auto-clear on browser close
  • Use DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) — Chrome: Settings → Security → Use secure DNS

Browser fingerprinting can identify you even without cookies. To reduce fingerprinting:

  • Use Firefox with privacy.resistFingerprinting enabled in about:config
  • Use Tor Browser for maximum anonymity
  • Keep your browser up to date — fingerprinting mitigations improve over time

Phishing Detection

Phishing is the most common method attackers use to steal credentials. Learning to spot it is critical.

Red Flags

  • Mismatched URLs: Hover over links before clicking. google.com.secure-login.evil.com is not Google
  • Urgent language: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" is a pressure tactic
  • Generic greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Unexpected attachments: Especially PDFs or Office docs requesting credentials
  • Poor grammar/spelling: Professional companies proofread their communications
  • Requests for credentials or 2FA codes: No legitimate service asks for these via email

Check the Link

# See where a shortened URL actually goes (without visiting)
curl -sI https://bit.ly/example | grep -i location

# Check a URL with VirusTotal
curl -s "https://www.virustotal.com/api/v3/urls" \
  -H "x-apikey: YOUR_API_KEY" \
  -F "url=https://example.com"
Never click links in unsolicited messages. Navigate to the site manually by typing the URL into your browser. If a bank email says your account has an issue, log in directly at your bank's website — do not use the link in the email.

Public Wi-Fi Safety

Public Wi-Fi (coffee shops, airports, hotels) is convenient but insecure. Anyone on the same network can potentially intercept your traffic.

Risks

  • Man-in-the-Middle (MITM): Attacker intercepts data between you and the server
  • Evil Twin: Fake Wi-Fi network that mimics a legitimate one
  • Packet sniffing: Unencrypted data (HTTP, FTP, SMTP) can be read by anyone

Protection

Method Effectiveness Notes
VPN (WireGuard/OpenVPN)HighEncrypts all traffic to VPN server
HTTPS-only browsingMediumOnly protects browser traffic
DNS-over-HTTPSLow-MediumHides DNS queries only
No public Wi-Fi (use mobile data)BestCellular data is more secure

Do not use "free Wi-Fi" portals that require you to log in with social media credentials — they are often data harvesting operations.

Social Engineering

Social engineering exploits human psychology, not technical vulnerabilities. Attackers pose as trusted individuals to extract information.

Common Tactics

  • Pretexting: Attacker fabricates a scenario (e.g., "I'm from IT support, we need your password")
  • Baiting: Leaving USB drives with malware in parking lots — never plug in unknown drives
  • Quid pro quo: Offering a "free security scan" in exchange for access
  • Tailgating: Following someone into a restricted area by pretending to be an employee
  • Vishing: Voice phishing over the phone — "This is your bank's fraud department"
  • Smishing: SMS phishing — fake package delivery notifications are common
Defense: Verify identity through a separate channel. If someone calls claiming to be your bank, hang up and call the number on the back of your card. Do not use any number they provide.

Device Updates

Keeping software updated is one of the simplest yet most effective security measures. Patches fix known vulnerabilities that attackers actively exploit.

# Debian/Ubuntu
sudo apt update && sudo apt upgrade -y

# Fedora
sudo dnf upgrade --refresh

# Arch
sudo pacman -Syu

# Flatpak
flatpak update

# Snap
sudo snap refresh

Account Recovery

What happens when you lose access? A solid recovery plan prevents permanent lockout.

SIM Swap Protection

SIM swapping is when an attacker convinces your mobile carrier to transfer your number to their SIM. This defeats SMS 2FA and can compromise all accounts tied to your phone number.

  • Set a PIN/password on your carrier account — call your provider and request a "port-out PIN"
  • Use TOTP or hardware keys instead of SMS for 2FA
  • Use a Google Voice number (US) or a virtual number as a secondary layer
  • Monitor your phone service — if your phone suddenly loses service, call your carrier immediately

Digital Footprint

Your digital footprint is the trail of data you leave online. Reducing it lowers your attack surface.

Audit Checklist

  • Search yourself — Google your name and common usernames. Remove what you can.
  • Delete unused accounts — use justdelete.me to find deletion instructions
  • Remove personal info from data brokers — services like DeleteMe can automate this
  • Review social media privacy settings — make profiles private, remove tagged posts
  • Check app permissions — revoke access for apps you no longer use (Google: myaccount.google.com/permissions)
  • Limit what you share publicly — birthday, address, phone number, family names
Privacy Tip: Use unique email aliases for every service. SimpleLogin and Firefox Relay create aliases that forward to your real inbox. If one leaks, you can disable just that alias.

Emergency Checklist

If you suspect you have been compromised, act quickly.

  1. Change passwords — start with your email account (it is the key to everything else)
  2. Enable or reset 2FA on all critical accounts
  3. Check active sessions and terminate unknown ones
  4. Scan for malware — run a full system scan with clamav or Windows Defender
  5. Remove unknown devices from trusted device lists
  6. Check forwarding rules — attackers often set up email forwarding to read your mail
  7. Notify your bank if financial accounts are involved
  8. File a report with the IC3 (US) or local cybercrime unit
  9. Run a breach check at haveibeenpwned.com
  10. Freeze your credit with the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) if identity theft is suspected
# ClamAV scan (Linux)
sudo clamscan -r --bell /home/username

# Check for open ports (possible backdoor)
sudo netstat -tulpn | grep LISTEN

# Check for SSH authorized_keys tampering
cat ~/.ssh/authorized_keys
Safety Warning: Verify any tool you use from official sources. Avoid third-party APK mirrors.
